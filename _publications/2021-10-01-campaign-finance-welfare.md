excerpt: 'Develops a model of political competition that studies the welfare effects of campaign-finance policy when contributions fund voter mobilization rather than advertising. Social costs are measured through reductions in the quality of public works. Public subsidies combined with contribution bans are welfare-improving only under low mobilization productivity, whereas contribution taxes that fund public projects prove welfare-improving under all technological conditions considered.'
